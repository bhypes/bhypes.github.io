My name is Brooke Hypes. I am currently a third-year PhD student in Planning, Governance, and Globalization at Virginia Tech. I position my work within critical governance studies with particular focus on Appalachia.

What does that mean, you ask?

Tautologically—I take a critical perspective on policy and governance.

In practice, this means I approach the study of policy and governance not simply as the academic study of how and why particular decisions have been made, but as a site where power operates through knowledge claims. Rather than assuming policy expertise is neutral or purely technical, I am interested in the social and political forces that determine whose knowledge is treated as credible and whose knowledge is dismissed.

That is: whose knowledge counts?

Why Appalachia?

Appalachia matters. The region is known for poverty, coalfields, and hillbilly stereotypes, but it has so much more—there are wonderful people trying to build communities and imagine a more just future together. Yet often, that work, and the needs driving it, are buried beneath dominant narratives about rural poverty and conservativism.

These narratives help make people in Appalachia the objects of policy interventions. As a result, they are often treated not as coequal, active participants in democratic life. My research interrogates this by examining how policy and governance structures are experienced by people living in Appalachian communities.

By examining whose knowledge is treated as credible—and whose is discounted altogether—I aim to make visible the epistemic injustices that shape policy outcomes in the region.

Appalachia is both a site and a lens: it grounds my work in concrete, place-based struggles while illuminating broader questions about the politics of knowledge.

But who are you, really?

Good question. My research is deeply rooted in Appalachia because I am deeply rooted in Appalachia. I grew up near Charleston, West Virginia, and spent every Sunday with my grandparents in Fayette County, eating my grandmother’s famous homemade biscuits and playing music (or, as we called it, “making noise”) with my grandfather. My family has lived in the area since it was part of Virginia, back when it was still a colony.

I love the things the region has to offer, from summer swimming to Bridge Day to winter hiking. I love the people here, too—they made me who I am today.

Reflexivity is central to my research. I firmly believe that all knowledge is perspectival; in this view, lack of so-called “objectivity” is only a problem if it goes unexamined.

Why I teach

I never thought I would find myself teaching. But now that I am, I never want to give it up.

For a long time, I thought the best teachers were those who knew everything and delivered detailed lectures from memory without a moment’s hesitation. But as I grew as a learner, I realized that was not necessarily the case. Expertise, confidence, and delivery are important—but not the most important.

Sincerity, authenticity, and humility do the most important work in the classroom. People know when you’re talking down to them, when you think you’re better than them, or when you assume they have nothing to offer. None of these things are conducive to learning.

From the very beginning, students must be embraced as co-creators of knowledge. They cannot learn to be participants in serious, important conversations if we don’t treat them as such.

In critical policy and governance studies, this is crucial. Dialogic pedagogy is not a methodological quirk—it is central to the discipline. Teaching is therefore not simply about conveying content, but about cultivating ways of thinking and being—and, ultimately, helping students develop as individuals who are prepared to participate in democratic communities.

My disciplinary journey

If you read my CV and wonder how it all fits together, look no further.

I received my BA in Political Science from Marshall University in August 2019. In October 2019, I began working for the West Virginia Legislature as a research analyst in the Performance Evaluation and Research Division, primarily conducting performance audits. I was truly blessed to have that position and to work as an oversight professional. They do important work.

For a time, I thought I would make a career in oversight—I even began an MPA program. Ultimately, however, I realized something was missing.

Then I enrolled in the Graduate Humanities Program at Marshall University, my alma mater. It was one of the best decisions I ever made. In addition to taking fascinating classes on everything from the West as a concept to Appalachian culture, I received excellent mentoring and made close friends I still have to this day. I would not be where I am now without the Graduate Humanities Program at Marshall.

My time in the program finally helped me name what I found lacking in oversight: reflexive consideration of epistemology. Performance auditing, in many ways, was about performing and documenting objectivity—a concept I had gradually grown disillusioned with, unsure if it could truly exist as it is often conceptualized.

And so I ended up in a doctoral program at Virginia Tech, studying epistemology in policy.

The rest, as they say, is history.
